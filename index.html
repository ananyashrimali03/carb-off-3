ASHRAE Design Competition: São Paulo Public Library

Project Overview

Building Type: Three-story public library
Location: São Paulo, Brazil
Climate Zone: 2A (Warm, Humid)
Service Life: 50 years
Team: Anisha, Ananya, Kansi, Shruti

Climate Characteristics

High temperature and humidity year-round, especially in summers. DBT comfort range: Winter (71°F–78°F), Summer (76°F–82°F). High humidity levels throughout the year drive the need for dedicated dehumidification strategies.

Performance Achievements

  • 45% energy reduction (284 to 157 kWh/m²)
  • 397,800 kWh/year solar generation
  • Operational carbon: 101 kgCO₂/m²

Primary HVAC System: VRF with DOAS

The Variable Refrigerant Flow (VRF) system with Dedicated Outdoor Air System (DOAS) was selected for its flexibility for future space modifications, individual zone control for occupant comfort, and quiet operation essential for a library environment — achieving 41–66% energy cost reduction compared to conventional systems.

System Components

  • DOAS-VRF Integration: Efficient temperature control through accessible ducted air distribution
  • Energy Recovery Ventilators (ERVs): Handle ventilation and humidity in high-occupancy areas (reading zones, study areas)
  • Desiccant Dehumidifiers: Critical for São Paulo's humid climate, ensuring stable humidity without over-reliance on cooling
  • Overhead Diffusers: Ceiling-mounted for even conditioned air distribution
  • Sidewall Grilles/Linear Diffusers: For perimeter zones near windows and heat sources

Zoning Strategy

Space-based zoning across reading areas, study zones, circulation, and administrative spaces. VRF outdoor unit placement optimized for system efficiency with multiple indoor units distributed per functional zone.

Photovoltaic System

  • Annual Generation: 397,800 kWh/year
  • System Capacity: 93 kW installed (92.8 kW calculated)
  • Panels: 232 panels @ 400W each (1.7 m² per panel)
  • Total Cost: 372,000 BRL (93 kW × 4,000 BRL/kW)
  • Avg Solar Radiation: 5.88 kWh/m²/day
  • Peak Month: September — 6.37 kWh/m²/day (34,886 kWh)

Additional Green Features

Green roof potential, bio-wall integration, and solar-assisted cooling enhancement complement the core HVAC strategy for a holistic low-carbon building approach.

Tools & Technologies

ClimateStudio, Energy Modeling, ASHRAE Standards, PVWatts, VRF-DOAS Design

Farmhouse Living: A Nostalgia Storytelling

Overview

This project explores how daylight can be orchestrated to evoke emotional memories and recreate nostalgic experiences. The design reimagines a family farmhouse gathering space in Central India, translating personal memories and a carefully curated music playlist into spatial lighting experiences using evidence-based daylighting metrics.

Design Concept

The space recreates cherished childhood memories of summer visits to a family farmhouse, where two families would gather to enjoy ice cream sundaes while listening to a shared playlist. The design hypothesis: daylight quality and distribution can be choreographed to parallel the emotional arc of music, creating spatial "verses," "choruses," and "intros" that correspond to different lighting qualities throughout the room.

Daylighting Performance Metrics

  • Spatial Daylight Autonomy (sDA): 57.1% — deliberate light imbalance, bright near balcony transitioning to subdued lighting near TV area
  • Annual Sunlight Exposure (ASE): 3.6% — excellent glare control minimizing overheating and excessive direct sunlight
  • Spatial Daylight Glare (sDG): 10.7% — strategic glare positioning creates intentional "imbalance" near windows
  • Illuminance Distribution: 300-3000 lux range across three distinct lighting zones

Musical Lighting Zones

The design translates a Bollywood-inspired playlist into architectural form, mapping musical characteristics to lighting qualities across three zones:

  • Intro Zone (near TV): Dim, dreamy environment below 300 lux — acoustic, gentle intros mapped to serene, dim entry lighting
  • Verse Zone (central): Moderately lit transitional space at 300-500 lux — reflective, melodious verses mapped to soft spectral light play
  • Chorus Zone (near balcony): Bright, warm space at 1000-3000 lux — heartfelt, warm choruses mapped to comforting light with views to nature

Design Narrative

Material palette includes exposed concrete, warm wood screens, Persian rugs, and strategic glazing oriented toward greenery, reinforcing the indoor-outdoor connection central to the farmhouse memory. The compact single-room approach reflects authentic memory — the families always gathered together in one space, honoring the intimacy and togetherness central to the nostalgic experience.

Tools & Technologies

ClimateStudio, Rhino, Grasshopper, Radiance, VR Visualization

Mapping Environmental Injustice in Cancer Alley

Overview

A spatial analysis of industrial burden, race, income, and poverty across Louisiana parishes. The 85-mile corridor along the Mississippi River between Baton Rouge and New Orleans contains over 150 petrochemical facilities concentrated in predominantly Black, low-income communities. This project investigates how historical land use decisions, industrial zoning practices, and racialized housing patterns created disproportionate environmental exposure.

Key Findings

  • Racial Disparity: Black residents within 1-3 miles of TRI facilities represent 34.58% of the population, exceeding the state average of 30.83%
  • Industrial Hotspots: Kernel density analysis reveals the most intense facility clusters in Ascension, St. James, St. John the Baptist, and St. Charles parishes
  • Multivariate Clustering: Five-cluster analysis shows Cancer Alley as a hotspot where environmental burden and racial inequity intersect most strongly
  • Core Conclusion: Environmental injustice is driven more by racial inequality than by purely economic disadvantage

Demographic Analysis

Choropleth mapping of racial composition reveals that parishes with higher Black population percentages overlap with regions of intense industrial activity. Within Cancer Alley, East Baton Rouge, St. John the Baptist, St. James, and Iberville show noticeably elevated percentages, underscoring environmental justice concerns.

Economic Vulnerability

Lower-income concentrations appear in northeastern and central parishes ($28,000-$40,000), while Cancer Alley parishes show moderate to moderately high income levels but remain surrounded by lower-income regions. Despite major industrial corridors, many Mississippi River communities fall below statewide upper income ranges.

Industrial Burden Analysis

The largest emission sources are heavily clustered along the Mississippi River corridor. Cancer Alley parishes exhibit both high facility density and the largest total-release values statewide, while rural and northern parishes show substantially lower cumulative toxic burden.

Kernel Density Estimation

Hotspot analysis reveals the most intense industrial clusters concentrated in the Mississippi River corridor, particularly in Ascension, St. James, St. John the Baptist, St. Charles, and Jefferson parishes. The spatial alignment of dense facility clusters with Cancer Alley underscores the disproportionate environmental burden carried by this region.

Exposure Proximity Analysis

  • 0-1 mile buffer: 208,925 residents, 33.38% Black population
  • 1-3 mile buffer: 851,877 residents, 34.58% Black population (highest)
  • 3-5 mile buffer: 878,981 residents, 32.76% Black population
  • Louisiana overall: 4,621,025 residents, 30.83% Black population

Multivariate Environmental & Social Risk Clustering

Integrated analysis combining industrial burden (mean toxic releases), poverty rate, median household income, and percent Black population across all Louisiana counties produces a five-cluster risk classification. Cancer Alley parishes consistently fall into the highest and moderate risk clusters, confirming that the region is not only an industrial landscape but also where environmental burden and racial inequity intersect most strongly.

Data Sources

Louisiana Counties Boundary Dataset, ACS Race Demographics, ACS Median Household Income, ACS Poverty Variables, US Major Rivers Polyline Dataset, EPA Toxic Release Inventory (TRI)

Tools & Technologies

ArcGIS Pro, ArcGIS StoryMaps, ModelBuilder, Python (ArcPy), Spatial Statistics, Kernel Density Estimation

Dog Jigsaw

Overview

A Flask web app that lets you browse random dog photos from the Dog CEO API, save your favorites, and solve 3×3 jigsaw puzzles made from them. Built using Claude Opus 4.6 as a development template.

Key Features

  • Browse Dogs: View random dog images, optionally filtered by breed
  • Save Favorites: Star photos to add them to your favorites collection
  • Jigsaw Puzzles: Play a 3×3 tile-swap puzzle using any favorited photo
  • Score Tracking: Earn points for solving puzzles (fewer moves = more points), tracked across sessions

How It Works

Browse random dog images on the home page and filter by breed with the dropdown. Click the star button to save a photo to your favorites. Go to Favorites and click the puzzle icon on any saved photo to play a jigsaw puzzle. Swap tiles by clicking two of them — solve the puzzle to earn points. Fewer moves = more points (max 100, min 10 per puzzle).

Demo

Project Structure

app.py — Flask routes and API logic
favorites.json — Saved favorite images (auto-created)
score.json — Puzzle score data (auto-created)
requirements.txt — Python dependencies
templates/ — index.html, favorites.html, puzzle.html

API Details

Uses the free Dog CEO API — no API key required. Endpoints include /breeds/list/all for breed listing and /breed/{breed}/images/random for fetching random images. All calls made via Python's requests module.

Tools & Technologies

Python, Flask, Dog CEO REST API, HTML/CSS/JS, Claude Opus 4.6

Carb-Off is a conversational AI carbon tracker inspired by Indore's proven behavior change model — the city that went from average cleanliness to #1 in India for 7 consecutive years through visible progress and collective identity.

Why Existing Apps Fail

  • Tedious Logging: Apps like Cool the Globe require navigating 1000+ dropdown menus
  • Meaningless Impact: Saving 2kg CO2 alone feels pointless
  • No Personalization: Same generic actions for everyone
  • Result: 80% abandonment within week 1

Our Solution

  • Conversational AI: Text your actions naturally, like talking to a friend
  • Personal Baseline: 2-minute onboarding calculates YOUR specific footprint
  • Live Collective Counter: Watch your actions join a global total that updates minute-by-minute
  • Cited Numbers: Every emission factor traces to EPA, DEFRA, or peer-reviewed research

The Indore Story

In 2016, my hometown Indore was an average city in terms of cleanliness. By 2023, it was the cleanest for the 7th year running. The secret wasn't fancy technology — it was visible progress (everyone could see rankings update in real-time) and collective identity (your actions joined something bigger). 3.5 million people changed behavior using this exact model, sustained for 7+ years.

How It Works

Onboarding: Tell the AI where you live, how you commute, what you eat, and your energy habits. It calculates your personal carbon baseline in 2 minutes.

Logging: Type naturally — "I took the bus today and had a veggie burrito for lunch" — and the AI calculates your savings with tangible equivalencies.

Collective Impact: Watch the live counter tick up as your savings join hundreds of others in real-time.

Why the LLM Is Not a Gimmick

The LLM handles three hard problems: intent parsing ("biked to work" → round trip or one way?), contextual calculation (auto-fills distance from user profile), and ambiguity resolution (asks natural clarifying questions). Critical rule: the LLM classifies and communicates — it NEVER generates carbon numbers. Every number traces to a cited emission factor from EPA 2024, DEFRA 2024, or Poore & Nemecek 2018.

Key Features (V0)

  • Conversational AI onboarding in 2 minutes — no forms
  • Natural language action logging — no dropdowns
  • Real-time collective counter updating every 5 seconds
  • 25+ emission factors sourced from EPA/DEFRA with citations

Tech Stack

Backend: FastAPI (Python), Claude Sonnet 4 (Anthropic AI), in-memory database (SQLite-ready)
Frontend: React (vanilla — no build step), mobile-first responsive design, real-time polling
Data: 25+ emission factors across food, transport, home energy, and lifestyle categories

Tools & Technologies

Environmental Performance Simulation & Daylighting Analysis

Small Office Space — Daylighting Study

Parametric daylighting analysis of a small office using Climate Studio, exploring spatial quality through annual metrics, glare analysis, and view studies across 3 design iterations.

Space Programming

  • Open Workspace (120 sq ft): High use intensity, high internal loads, priority 10/10
  • Lounge (70 sq ft): Medium use intensity, low internal loads, priority 8/10
  • Conference (50 sq ft): Low use intensity, low internal loads, priority 7/10
  • All spaces: Cooling and ventilation as HVAC priority

Daylighting Results (Climate Studio)

  • sDA₃₀₀/₅₀%: 57.1% — just above threshold for LEED credit
  • ASE₁₀₀₀,₂₅₀: 3.6% — good control over excessive direct sunlight
  • Average UDIa: 54.2%
  • Average Lux: 780
  • Blinds Open: 92.7% of the time

Spatial Light Zones — Design Intent

"Chorus" zone (near balcony/windows, 300–3000 lux): Heartfelt, warm, comforting
"Verse" zone (central areas, 300–500 lux): Reflective, introspective, nostalgic, melodious
"Intro" zone (dim zones near TV, below 300 lux): Dreamy, serene, gentle, calm

Creates a dull indie-like vibe for rest and relaxation — bright near outdoor connection, intimate interior. Perfect balance for residential comfort.

Glare & Illuminance Analysis

  • Spatial Daylight Glare (sDG): 10.7% of space experiences glare — contained to specific viewing angles near windows
  • Near Windows: 1000–3000 lux (red/yellow zones)
  • Central Areas: 300–500 lux (green zones)
  • Darker Areas: <300 lux (blue zones)
  • Mitigation: Needed in specific zones only, not whole room

Secondary School Building — Pittsburgh (IESVE Analysis)

Climate Zone: 5A (Pittsburgh)
Software: IESVE (Integrated Environmental Solutions Virtual Environment)
Standards: ASHRAE 90.1-2016, PNNL guidelines
Categories: Envelope geometry, envelope thermal properties, internal loads, operational schedules

WWR Optimization

  • Baseline (35% WWR): 14,251.9 mBtu/yr — EUI 33.0 kBtu/ft²/yr
  • Iteration 1 (30%): 13,386.1 mBtu/yr — EUI 31.0
  • Iteration 2 (25%, Best): 8,835.4 mBtu/yr — EUI 20.9
  • Finding: 25% optimal based on daylighting requirements + energy performance

Roof & Wall Thermal Properties

  • Roof: Baseline R-15 (U-0.067) maintained — R-18 and R-30 iterations showed minimal EUI change; PRM CZ5 standards already optimized
  • Wall Baseline: Steel Frame 2×6, R-13 + R-3 continuous (U-0.084) — EUI 33.0
  • Wall Best (R-19): Steel Frame 16" o.c., R-19 cavity (U-0.109) — EUI 20.9
  • ASHRAE Requirement: U-value ≤ 0.084 Btu/(h·ft²·°F) for above-grade walls

Slab & Glazing Properties

  • Slab Baseline: R-8 + R-9 continuous (R-total 18.18, U-0.051) — EUI 33.0
  • Slab Best: Max insulation (R-total 40.95, U-0.02) — EUI 20.8 (significant improvement)
  • Glazing Baseline: Double clear/tint (R-1.75, U-0.570, SHGC 0.490) — EUI 33.0
  • Glazing Best (Non-metal frame): R-3.23, U-0.310, SHGC 0.380, VT 0.420 — EUI 19.0
  • Critical Finding: Glazing made the BIGGEST difference in EUI. Non-metal framing superior to metal

Lighting Load Optimization

  • Baseline: DF 80%, LPD 1.05 BTU/h·ft² — EUI 19.0
  • Iteration 1: DF 70%, LPD 1.00 — EUI 17.0 (~5% energy reduction)
  • Iteration 2 (Best): DF 60%, LPD 0.80 — EUI 15.0 (~12.3% savings vs. baseline)
  • Guidelines: DF recommended 60–85% for secondary schools; LPD should be 25–30% below ASHRAE 90.1
  • Combined Effect: Direct lighting energy reduced + cooling loads decreased (less heat from fixtures). Slight trade-off: increased heating in cold months

Final Optimization Summary — Parametric Flow

  • Step 1 — WWR 25%: EUI → 20.9
  • Step 2 — Roof (baseline maintained): EUI → ~20.9
  • Step 3 — Wall R-19: EUI → 20.9
  • Step 4 — Slab (max insulation): EUI → 20.8
  • Step 5 — Glazing (non-metal, Low-E): EUI → 19.0
  • Step 6 — Lighting (DF 60%, LPD 0.80): EUI → 15.0
  • Total Reduction: 33.0 → 15.0 kBtu/ft²/yr (54.5% improvement)

Climate Analysis Integration

Pittsburgh Climate Strategy

  • Prevailing winds from west — higher insulation needed on west facade
  • Window orientation: South (max winter sun), North (minimal to reduce heat loss)
  • Shading: Operable external louvers/overhangs on south for seasonal adjustment
  • Wall: High-performance insulation, air sealing, vapor barriers, thermal mass
  • Roof: High R-value, light-colored/cool materials for summer reflection

Miami Climate Strategy

  • Summer winds leveraged for natural ventilation
  • Reduce southern radiation in summer, increase indoor sun in winter
  • Hurricane and flood protection integral to design
  • Wall: Reinforced concrete with insulation (structural + thermal mass)
  • Roof: High-performance insulation, light-colored to reduce heat pooling
  • Glazing: Low SHGC, balance daylight vs. harsh sun, Low-E coatings

Building Performance Modeling — Pittsburgh & Miami Comparison

Project Scope & Methodology

Software: eQUEST 3.65 (DOE-2 engine)
Team: 4-person team (2 on Pittsburgh, 2 on Miami)
Approach: One-Factor-At-A-Time (OFAT) parametric optimization
Total Models: 37 energy models (including baselines and iterations)
Analysis Method: Schematic Design Wizard mode
Building Type: Office building
Climate Zones: Pittsburgh (5A — Cold), Miami (1A — Hot-Humid)
Baseline: Identical for both cities to enable direct comparison

Pittsburgh Analysis (Climate Zone 5A)

Cold winters with heating demand, moderate summers, prevailing winds from west. Focus on minimizing winter heat loss through thermal insulation, passive solar heating, and air sealing.

Pittsburgh — Parametric Optimization Sequence

  • Orientation: Baseline already optimal — no EUI change
  • Building Form: 5 iterations tested — rectangle maintained as best performer (EUI 43.94)
  • WWR: Reduced from 40% to 22.5% — EUI 43.94 → 43.1 kBtu/ft²/yr (most significant geometric parameter)
  • Wall Assembly: Steel Frame 16" o.c., R-19 cavity — EUI dropped to 40.3
  • Roof Assembly: R-18 baseline maintained — minimal EUI change (40.06)
  • Slab Assembly: Increased insulation R-8→4.6", R-9→5.5" — EUI 20.80 (significant improvement, limited by economic viability)
  • Glazing (Most Impactful): ASHRAE CZ5 non-metal frame, Quad Low-E — EUI dropped to 19.0

Pittsburgh — Glazing Comparison

  • Baseline (Double clear/tint): R-1.75, U-0.570, SHGC 0.490, VT 0.520 — EUI 33.0
  • ASHRAE CZ5 Metal frame: R-2.63, U-0.380, SHGC 0.370, VT 0.420 — EUI 20.0
  • ASHRAE CZ5 Non-metal frame (Best): R-3.23, U-0.310, SHGC 0.380, VT 0.420 — EUI 19.0

Pittsburgh — Final Results

  • Baseline EUI: 43.94 kBtu/ft²/yr | Cost: $39,713/yr | CO₂: 1,117.57 metric tons
  • Optimized EUI: 41.5 kBtu/ft²/yr (-5.6%) | Cost: $37,063/yr ($2,650 savings) | CO₂: 1,051.24 metric tons
  • CO₂ Reduction: 66.33 metric tons/year (6.7% decrease)
  • Best Config: Rectangle, 22.5% WWR, Wood Frame R-5, 8" Concrete Roof R-21, Under-Slab R-10, Quad Low-E Krypton

Pittsburgh — Benchmarking

National Benchmark: 82.8 kBtu/ft²/yr | Pennsylvania Avg: 61.8 kBtu/ft²/yr
Baseline: 43.95 kBtu/ft²/yr (47% better than national)
Optimized: 33.25 kBtu/ft²/yr (60% better than national)

Miami Analysis (Climate Zone 1A)

Hot, humid year-round with high cooling demand. Hurricane and flood considerations. Focus on reducing solar heat gain, managing humidity, and passive solar design (window shading achieved 24.4% comfort improvement).

Miami — Form Optimization (6 Iterations)

  • Baseline Rectangle: EUI 48.34 | Cost $113,609
  • Trapezoid (Winner): EUI 48.12 | Cost $113,098 — angled walls reduce solar heat gain
  • U-shape: EUI 50.50 | T-shape: EUI 50.26 | Plus: EUI 51.02 | L-shape: EUI 50.50

Miami — Directional WWR Optimization

  • North: 25% (reduced from 40%)
  • South: 22% (maximum reduction for solar control)
  • East: 20% (morning sun mitigation)
  • West: 18% (lowest — harsh afternoon sun)
  • Strategy: Directional optimization vs. uniform reduction; South and West prioritized for heat gain reduction

Miami — Envelope Optimization

  • Wall (Optimized): 12" HW Concrete with R-21 exterior + R-21 interior — thermal mass + hurricane resistance
  • Roof (Optimized): Clay Tile, White lacquer (reflective) + R-18 + R-19 batt — light color significantly reduced heat penetration
  • Slab: Vinyl Tile, 12" Concrete — low conductivity, high density materials
  • Glazing: Quad Low-E, 1/8" clear, 1/3" Krypton (4651), Wood/Aluminum Clad frame

Miami — Roof Iteration Analysis

  • Baseline: Built-up Medium, R-18 — EUI 48.34, Cost $111,095
  • Iteration 1 (Best): Clay Tile White gloss, R-18 + R-21 batt — EUI 45.57, Cost $104,567
  • Iteration 2: Built-up Medium, R-21 + R-21 batt — EUI 45.68, Cost $104,822
  • Iteration 3: Built-up Medium, R-12, no batt — EUI 45.75, Cost $104,982

Miami — Final Results

  • Baseline EUI: 48.34 kBtu/ft²/yr | Cost: $111,095/yr | CO₂: 440,516 lbs
  • Optimized EUI: 44.08 kBtu/ft²/yr (-8.8%) | Cost: $101,048/yr ($10,047 savings) | CO₂: 410,984 lbs
  • CO₂ Reduction: 29,532 lbs/year (6.7% decrease)
  • Best Config: Trapezoid, N:25%/S:22%/E:20%/W:18% WWR, 12" Concrete R-21+R-21, Clay Tile White R-18+R-19, Quad Low-E Krypton

Comparative Conclusions

Pittsburgh vs. Miami — Key Achievements

  • Pittsburgh: 5.6% EUI reduction | $2,650/yr savings | 66.33 metric tons CO₂/yr | Best strategies: WWR reduction, high-performance glazing, slab insulation
  • Miami: 8.8% EUI reduction | $10,047/yr savings (4× Pittsburgh) | 29,532 lbs CO₂/yr | Best strategies: Trapezoid form, directional WWR, reflective roofing, concrete thermal mass

Critical Insights

  • WWR and glazing had the greatest impact across both climates
  • Form matters more in cooling-dominated climates (Miami trapezoid vs. Pittsburgh rectangle)
  • Miami showed larger % gains due to higher baseline consumption
  • Material selection must respond to climate: concrete mass for Miami heat, insulation for Pittsburgh cold
  • Both cities achieved significant improvements through climate-specific strategies

Tools & Technologies

eQUEST 3.65 (DOE-2), ASHRAE Standards, Parametric OFAT Analysis, Energy Benchmarking

Museum of Event 1971 — Bangladesh Liberation War Memorial

Project Overview

Type: Thesis Project — Cultural/Memorial Architecture
Duration: 4 Months
Location: Taki, West Bengal, India (India-Bangladesh border on River Ichhamati)
Recognition: Awarded "Best Thesis 2023"
Inspirations: Louis I. Kahn, Charles Correa, Peter Zumthor, Juhani Pallasmaa

Design Philosophy

Architectural storytelling of the 1971 Bangladesh Liberation War through phenomenological design, creating an experiential journey that transitions users through emotions chronologically as historical events unfolded. The museum uses Gordon Cullen's Serial Vision — sequential discovery, moments of surprise, and sensory manipulation through water, light, and shadow.

Site Selection — Strategic Criteria

  • Migration corridors along eastern Radcliffe line (India–East Pakistan border)
  • River border location — Ichhamati River acts as international boundary
  • Cultural significance — Durga Puja festival, December 16 boat memorial (India-Bangladesh victory celebration)
  • Metropolitan accessibility — weekend getaway from Kolkata
  • Cross-border relations — shared remembrance strengthening ties

Key Metrics

  • Total Site Area: 41,682 sqm
  • Landscape Coverage: 33,353 sqm (80%)
  • Ground Coverage: 7,004 sqm | FAR: 0.32
  • Total Travel Distance: 1.2 km
  • Journey Time: 45–60 minutes
  • Zones: 12 emotional zones across 2 museum blocks

North Museum Block — Bangladesh Perspective (6 Blocks)

Narrative: Before the War → West Pakistan Dominance → Genocide → Awami League Win → Liberation Attempt → Attempt to Escape
Character: Closed envelope, limited calculated light, dark/calm/cool atmosphere
Materials: Rat-trap bond double-wall system using local brick from neighboring factories

North Block — Key Spaces

  • Hall of Trivial Essence: Bamboo jaali shadows with water reflection create contemplative atmosphere
  • Hall of Dominance: Conscious pressure visualization through spatial compression
  • Hall of Unstability: Sloped walkways toward central water, gravitational pull sensation
  • Hall of Genocide: 3 million hanging spherical objects (representing deaths) + 40,000 pebbles on floor (representing women assaulted)
  • Spatial Strategy: Failed circulation — stairs that lead nowhere, suppression through dead-ends

South Museum Block — Indian Perspective (6 Blocks)

Narrative: Indian Support → Jessore Liberation → Upper Hand → Operation Python → Battles → Victory
Character: Open envelope, multiple light sources, bright/warm/energetic atmosphere
Strategy: Progressive liberation through increasing openness

South Block — Key Spaces

  • Staircase of Reunion: 4 entrances with slits providing glimpses, frequency increases to reunite families
  • Liberation of Jessore: Exterior staircase indicating geographical liberation
  • Battle of Chamb: Exterior entry/exit (Southern Kashmir captured by Pakistan)
  • Martyrs' Hall: Surrounded by waterbody reflecting light onto interior sculptures
  • Gallery Spaces: Sharp sunlight for wayfinding, energetic progression

Central Space — Circle of Stillness (Path of Exodus)

  • Concept: Serene circular waterbody represents peace/destination
  • Below-grade tunnel separates chaos from peace (spatial segregation)
  • Wide entrance (mass exodus) → congested exit (arrival in India with resource shortages)
  • Skylights that mislead direction — disorientation during migration
  • Dead-end branches — representing deaths during exodus
  • Perimeter wall: Symmetric, non-dynamic view for contemplation

Building Performance & Climate Response

Solar orientation: East-west axis aligned with sunrise/sunset for symbolic "birth of new nation"
Sunpath optimization: Waterbody positioned to create reflections during key times of day
Natural ventilation: Open central space above grade with below-grade exodus tunnel to separate thermal zones
Daylighting: North Block (dark, controlled) vs. South Block (bright, open envelope)
Passive shading: Local bamboo jaali (lattice) creates dynamic shadow patterns — direct sunlight, still water reflection, dynamic water reflections

Material Strategy

  • Structure: Framed structure with rat-trap bond double walls
  • Brick: Standard brick from neighboring local factories
  • Jaali Screens: Local bamboo craft — controlled light, cultural authenticity, dynamic shadows, natural ventilation
  • Water Features: Still and dynamic bodies for reflection, sound, thermal regulation, and symbolic meaning

Light & Shadow Studies

North Block (Dark Narrative): Minimal southern openings, strategic northern placement, orderly shadows, broken mirror reflections showing "shattered lives", jaali creates moving shadows through water reflection.

South Block (Bright Narrative): Maximum southern exposure, large waterbody amplifies light, multiple pause points to combat museum fatigue, energetic warm atmosphere.

Experimental Work: Physical models testing bamboo jaali shadow patterns — direct sunlight effects, still water reflections, dynamic water reflections integrated into museum design.

Serial Vision — Design Methodology

Gordon Cullen's Serial Vision applied: existing view → emerging view → sequential discovery → moments of surprise → sensory manipulation (water sounds without direct visibility). Building heights correspond to emotional intensity of events. Sky captured in architectural frames. Landscape enhanced through reflection.

Tools & Technologies

3D Modeling, Solar Analysis, Perspectival Framing, Rendering, Physical Model Making, Phenomenological Design

Tesseract — Mixed-Use Development in Kolkata

Project Overview

Type: Studio Project — Mixed-Use (Commercial + Residential)
Duration: 3 Months
Location: Downtown Kolkata, India
Climate: Hot-Humid Tropical
Recognition: Top 5 in External Jury Critique

Design Concept — The Fourth Dimension

The "Tesseract" metaphor extends architecture beyond 3D (closed and open spaces) into a 4th dimension: intangible culture development through built form. The building acts as a catalyst for community formation — physical networking as architecture's fourth dimension.

Climate-Responsive Form Development

Iterative Parametric Process

  • Step 1 — Basic Blocking: 4 apartments per block, 20 blocks total. Regular stacking for two-sided exposure
  • Step 2 — Dislocation: Blocks shifted according to wind flow direction. Improved cross-ventilation, some units gain 3+ facade exposures
  • Step 3 — Porosity (Final): Multiple climate software iterations. Venturi effect optimization — wind speeds up through building voids. Most space falls within comfort zone

Wind Flow Strategy

Void openings aligned with prevailing wind direction (especially summer months). Natural cooling through accelerated airflow. Inner open spaces shaded most of day throughout year. Reduced mechanical cooling loads through passive ventilation.

Building Program

Vertical Stacking (12+ Floors)

  • Basement: Parking for residents, office workers, and retail visitors. Direct vertical access to residential (privacy maintained)
  • Ground + 1st Floor: Retail shopping complex — extroverted, street-facing. Two anchor stores at opposite ends force movement through external shops
  • 2nd Floor: Collaborative office/coworking spaces with flexible layouts
  • 3rd Floor (Podium): Residential amenity deck — sports, pool, sauna, community hall, guest rooms, open terraces
  • Floors 4–8: 3BHK Simplex apartments (living, dining, 3 bedrooms, 2 baths, kitchen, balconies)
  • Floors 9–12+: 4BHK Duplex apartments (two-level units with master suite, private terrace, study)
  • Total: 80 apartments across 4 vertical residential cores + 2 separate commercial cores

Community-Building Through Design

Activity Landscape — 15+ Node Types

  • Agricultural Farms + Urban Sea Farm (rooftop aquaponics)
  • Sensory Spaces: Meditation, reflection zones
  • Reading Spaces + Hobby Spaces (craft, art, maker)
  • Food Trucks + Communal Dining
  • Exercising Nodes: Fitness, yoga, sports
  • Playscapes + Open Seating + Gathering Nodes
  • Energy Generation: Solar panel integration

Design Psychology

Smaller volumes = better engagement — research shows people interact more in compact spaces. Shared activities = common ground — similar interests create conversation and bonds. Reward-based destinations — coffee shops, gardens as "rewards" for social trips. Multiple engagement points attract diverse residents.

Urban Design — "The Street Isn't Curb to Curb"

Ground level: Setback activation with public architecture on lower levels, breaking the rigid compound wall concept.
"At 4th floor we lose identity with streets" — public spaces on floors 0–3 for street connection, private residences floor 4+ for disconnect from noise.
Marketing through visibility: When people see others enjoying services, they're attracted — better than banners.

Building Performance

  • Thermal: Porous form reduces urban heat island. Wind acceleration through voids (Venturi effect). Shaded central spaces throughout day
  • Ventilation: 100% of units with cross-ventilation (minimum 2 facades exposed)
  • Daylighting: Multiple facade exposures per unit. Open central voids prevent deep, dark cores
  • Energy: Natural ventilation reduces mechanical cooling. Green roofs for thermal mass. Solar panels on activity platforms

Sustainability Features

  • Passive: Wind-driven natural ventilation, solar orientation, green roofs, vertical landscaping, urban farming, shaded outdoor spaces
  • Active: Solar panels, rainwater harvesting, gray water recycling, energy-efficient lighting, smart building controls
  • Social: Affordable housing through efficient design, mixed-income potential, community spaces foster social capital, local employment

Circulation & Privacy

Non-monotonous corridor layouts with private entrance zones per apartment. Visual variety breaks repetition. 4 residential cores + 2 commercial cores (completely separate). Basement provides direct access to residences only — privacy maintained between residential and commercial users. Unique unit placements enable varied pricing flexibility.

Computational Design Process

Iterative optimization: Functional stacking (baseline) → wind-responsive dislocation → climate software validation → porosity refinement → activity space integration → final form.
Tools: Climate analysis software (wind flow + comfort validation), 3D modeling (Rhino/SketchUp), daylighting analysis (shadow studies for void placement), conceptual CFD for Venturi effect validation.

Tools & Technologies

Climate Analysis, CFD (Conceptual), 3D Modeling, Daylighting Analysis, Parametric Form Development, Urban Design

Social Nexus — Tianducheng Ghost City Activation

Project Overview

Type: International Competition
Duration: 54 hours (timed competition)
Team: 3 members
Location: Tianducheng, China (Parisian replica / "duplitecture")
Recognition: International Winner — Docexdoce Competition
Publication: Web publication

Problem Statement

Tianducheng is a Chinese suburb replicating Paris (including an Eiffel Tower replica), resulting in ghost town conditions — used only as a photo backdrop with informal, unstructured living, no cultural identity, and a small growing population with zero community engagement. The challenge: create cultural identity through intangible daily activities rather than iconic architecture replication.

Design Philosophy

"Culture does not make people, people make culture" — Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie

Replicating heritage buildings does not equal cultural authenticity. Cultural identity comes from the intangible aspects of daily resident activities. Architecture serves as a catalyst, not the solution. The design responds through a simple, effective grid system with adaptive squares enabling sense of ownership, developing community through three pillars: interaction, collaboration, and creation.

Site Strategy — Central Park Transformation

  • Location: Central park at base of Eiffel Tower replica
  • Step 1 — Baseline Grid: Simple pathways dividing park into regular squares
  • Step 2 — Shape Variation: Cut into different sized/shaped plots
  • Step 3 — Community Customization: Residents build collaborative environments in each plot

16 Adaptive Square Typologies

  • Social/Gathering: Gathering nodes, open seating spaces, communal spaces (shared meals, events)
  • Recreation: Mini playgrounds, exercise circuits, playscapes (creative play)
  • Learning/Working: Reading spaces, hobby spaces (craft, art, making), mini farm (urban agriculture education)
  • Productivity: Agricultural farms, agrofarms (specialized crops), urban sea farm (aquaculture)
  • Infrastructure: Food trucks (mobile dining, entrepreneurship), forklift charging stations, energy generation (solar, community power)
  • Wellness: Sensory spaces (meditation, mindfulness)

Design Principles

  • Flexibility: Grid adapts to any combination of activities. No predetermined program — residents choose functions based on community needs. Phased development possible
  • Community Ownership: Residents develop their own grids, creating territorial ownership and pride in community-built environment
  • Social Psychology: Activities requiring interaction, collaboration, creation. Similar interest groups form naturally. Shared activities = conversation topics = stronger bonds

Urban Strategy

Circulation: Grid pathways ensure connectivity with multiple access points to each square. Pedestrian-priority environment with flexible routing.

Landscape: Green infrastructure in every grid with varied topography, water features, and native planting.

Infrastructure: Underground utilities, distributed water/power to each grid. Modular infrastructure allows phased development with sustainable drainage systems.

Expected Outcomes — Phased Development

  • Phase 1 (Immediate): Activity kickstart for residents, initial community formation, testing of popular activity types
  • Phase 2 (Medium-term): Organic expansion of successful activities, resident-driven modifications, emerging neighborhood identities
  • Phase 3 (Long-term): Established cultural practices, unique Tianducheng identity (not Parisian replica), intangible heritage development, ghost town transformation

Tools & Technologies

Urban Design, Adaptive Grid Systems, Community Participation Design, Landscape Architecture, Phased Development Strategy

La Gracieux — Cannes Film Festival Pavilion

Project Overview

Type: International Competition
Team: 3 members
Location: Plage Long Beach, Cannes, France
Program: Festival pavilion + pop-up cinema
Recognition: International Top 30 — Archasm Competition
Publication: Web publication

Design Philosophy

"Architecture and filmmaking, both should be able to create a narrative other than mainstream, in order to be incredibly successful."

Architecture and filmmaking are treated as parallel narrative mediums. Three elements of storytelling — Character, Conflict, and Desire — are translated directly into architectural form, creating a pavilion that visitors experience as a story unfolding.

Architectural Translation of Filmmaking

  • Character — The Spine: Cubicle volume with only structural spine retained. Hollow yet looks large — visual trick through skeletal structure. Creates iconic silhouette on beach, porous allowing views through. Minimal material, maximum presence
  • Conflict — Level Changes: Series of level differences throughout pavilion. Visitors surpass many obstacles to reach destination. Ramps, stairs, platforms at varied heights. Physical effort mirrors emotional investment in a film's plot
  • Desire — Elevated Seating: Cinema seating elevated from ground achieves viewer desire to be "above" while watching. Panoramic views of beach + screen. The destination worth the journey's effort

Spatial Organization

  • Showcase Space: Exhibition area for festival content at entry level
  • Convergence Zone: Social gathering and networking at mid-level
  • Souvenir Shop: Festival merchandise along the journey
  • Ramp/Circulation: Meandering path creating gradual ascent + anticipation
  • Cinema Seating: Elevated viewing area — the "desire" destination
  • Support Spaces: Restrooms, storage, technical facilities

Volumetric Strategy

Volume: Create desire through elevated seating platform.
Spine: Exclude all faces, retain only structural skeleton — maximum visual impact with minimum material.
Skin: Cover select volumes (museum, ramp, café) with tensile fabric for weather protection while maintaining porosity.

Structural System

  • Frame: Triangulated truss system — lightweight steel, prefabricated components
  • Temporary construction: Designed for rapid assembly and complete disassembly post-festival
  • Skin: Tensile fabric for shaded areas
  • Platforms: Timber decking for seating and walkways
  • Sustainability: Minimal foundations (beach site), fully recyclable/reusable materials, minimal environmental impact

Experiential Journey

Approach: Skeletal character visible from distance on beach → Entry: Ground-level showcase space → Ascent: Ramp creates gradual elevation + anticipation → Convergence: Social gathering node at mid-level → Souvenir: Commemorative stop before cinema → Arrival: Elevated cinema seating with ocean view and sunset framing.

Climate Response — Coastal Environment

Sun: Tensile fabric skins provide targeted shade.
Wind: Porous frame allows sea breeze through — natural ventilation.
Rain: Protected areas under fabric skins.
Heat: Elevated platforms catch sea breeze, thermal mass minimized for temporary use.
Views: Beach-facing transparency, city-facing enclosure. Elevated platforms frame ocean + sunset.

Cultural Context

The Cannes Film Festival celebrates the collective effort of creative people. Audio-visual experience requires synchronization of senses. The pavilion becomes a "buildification" of story — spaces transition through drama and sophistication phases, with architecture evoking emotion in parallel to film.

Tools & Technologies

3D Modeling, Structural Design, Tensile Fabric Systems, Temporary Construction, Narrative Architecture, Experiential Design

Fractal — Modular Housing for Migrant Workers

Overview

Pandemic response architecture addressing the COVID-19 housing crisis for urban poor and migrant workers. During lockdown, millions of migrant workers were stranded in cities — unable to return home, without transportation, spending months in goods carrier trucks and streets with complete loss of income and shelter.

Recognition: Shortlisted — IGBC (Indian Green Building Council)

Design Concept: "Fractal"

Named after the mathematical concept of self-similar patterns at any scale. The system works identically for a sole family or a dense community — scalable from 1 unit to 100+ units while maintaining livability at all scales. A flexible prefabricated initiative that blends in any context.

Why Hexagonal Geometry?

Psychological Benefits: Reduces feeling of "packed closeness," provides more atmosphere for 24/7 indoor stay, psychologically more comfortable shape, less claustrophobic than rectangular boxes.

Functional Benefits: Optimized daylight exposure with more facade area per unit, better ventilation from multiple facade orientations, spatial efficiency fitting more units in same area, circulation flexibility with no dead ends, and layout that promotes socialization.

Environmental Benefits: Maximum outer facade area for daylighting, better natural ventilation from multiple angles, reduced mechanical cooling needs, and integration of community gardens between units.

Module Design — The Kit

Flat-Pack Delivery System:

  • Structural frame: Timber/metal interlocking members
  • Floor plates: Modular flooring sections
  • Wall panels: Shutters/covering with insulation
  • Roof components: Weatherproof covering
  • Joineries: Connectors, fasteners, hardware

Materials: Paper, wood, straw (sustainable, affordable). Fabric walls for flexibility, natural insulation (straw, cardboard), residents can customize with local materials.

Assembly Process

Step 1 — Interlocking: Frame members slot together using joinery system requiring no specialized tools. Residents can self-assemble with basic instruction.

Step 2 — Finishing: Structure + covering material put over frame. Residents customize screens/walls. Personalization creates sense of home.

Step 3 — Combining: Units combine in various honeycomb geometries with infinite arrangement possibilities. Shared walls reduce material needs, community develops organically.

Scalability & Flexibility

Individual Family (1 Unit): Single hexagonal module, all sides open to exterior, maximum ventilation/daylight.

Small Community (5-10 Units): Honeycomb cluster with shared central courtyard, community garden spaces between units.

Dense Community (50+ Units): Multi-story hexagonal towers with vertical stacking, shared circulation, roof gardens, integrated community spaces.

Urban Integration

High-Density Cities (Mumbai): Vertical towers in gaps between buildings, near parking, in unused lots, on underutilized rooftops of existing buildings.

Semi-Urban Areas: Horizontal clusters with community spaces.

Rural Areas: Scattered individual units near work sites.

Tower Configuration: Ground level (community kitchen, storage), mid-levels (residential units), roof (garden, gathering space, water collection).

Community Spaces Integration

Between units: community gardens, children's play areas, shared cooking facilities, learning/skill-sharing spaces, storage.

"Tower as Second Place": Learning opportunities, skill development workshops, social support network, mental health through community connection.

Psychological Design Considerations

Identity in Crisis: Customization opportunity through personalized fabric/screens, traditional cultural motif integration, sense of ownership from self-building, community formation through shared construction.

Comfort in Confinement: Hexagon reduces claustrophobia vs. rectangle, multiple facade angles provide varied views, natural ventilation ensures better air quality, community visibility reduces isolation.

Sustainability

Materials: Paper, wood, straw — renewable, biodegradable, minimal carbon footprint, locally sourceable, affordable.

Construction: No heavy machinery, minimal skilled labor, self-assembly by residents, temporary/reversible foundations.

Operation: Natural ventilation (no mechanical cooling), daylighting, rainwater collection, community gardens for food security.

End-of-Life: Fully demountable, components reusable, biodegradable materials, zero waste disassembly.

Logistics & Implementation

Transport: Flat-pack design, all components fit in conventional truck, lightweight (manual handling), many units per truck.

Assembly: Simple tools (hammer, screwdriver), pictorial instruction manual, 2-4 people can assemble 1 unit in 4-6 hours.

Distribution: Government/NGO procurement, distribution through relief networks, site selection by local authorities, residents self-assemble with supervision.

Quantitative Impact

Each unit: ~12-15 sqm living space | 1 truck: ~20-30 flat-pack units | 1 tower (100 units): ~100 families housed | Assembly: 1 community (10 units) = 2-3 days with 10 people

Tools & Technologies

Prefabricated Timber Systems, Hexagonal Geometry, Modular Construction, Sustainable Materials, Flat-Pack Design, Community-Driven Assembly, Pandemic Response Architecture
